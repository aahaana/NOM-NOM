Food Science
"Food Science is a broad concept used to describe the application of scientific principles
to create and maintain a wholesome food supply."

Just as society has evolved over time, our food system has also evolved into complexity. It is a crucial need of the hour to educate new generations about the science that revolves around one of our most basic needs, food, and about the commitment of food science and technology contributing to healthier people everywhere, especially now, with the prevalence of over-the-counter processed foods infested with lifestyle diseases. At Nom Nom, we believe in educating our generations about the basics of cooking ingredients with mindful awareness to imbibe their highest value possible and translate food into nutrition. Here are some handy tips you can inculcate while cooking your meals.

Fruits

Fruits

• You may sprinkle some lemon juice on your bowl of fruits to prevent browning. The citrus nature of lemon helps reverse the process of oxidation and keeps fruits fresh for long.


• Eat with the seasons – this is nature’s way of making sure our bodies get a healthy mix of nutrients and plant chemicals.


• To make your fresh berries last longer, dunk them in hot water as soon as you get home.

Vegetables

• As soon as you drain your boiled veggies, coat them in oil to prevent them from drying out.


• Leafy green vegetables such as spinach and bibb, romaine, red leaf, and other types of lettuce will stay fresher longer if you rinse them in cool water before refrigerating.


• Ginger, also called ginger root, can last in your fridge for a few weeks. To keep it longer, toss it in your freezer.

Dairy

• Shredded cheese from the block always melts better than sliced cheese, due to increased surface area and contact with heat.


• Baking cakes or other sweets with yogurt adds moisture and tang. Because yogurt is thick, you will need less flour in baked goods, producing less gluten and a softer texture.


• Grass-fed dairy is healthier and generally more climate-friendly than grain-fed dairy.

Meat

Meat

• Let the meat rest for 15-20 minutes before cutting into it for fibers to start soaking up some of the moisture; less moisture loss once you slice into it.


• Use high heat to develop flavor, while use low heat to preserve moisture.


• Meat should be removed when it's 5-10 degrees below the desired serving temperature to aid carryover-cooking.

Seafood

Seafood

• Fresh seafood should smell fresh and mild, not fishy or sour. A smell like ammonia means it’s spoiled and you shouldn’t eat it.


• Cook seafood to an internal temperature of 145°.


• Fish is ready when the meat is opaque and separates easily with a fork. Clams, mussels, and oysters are ready when the shells open during cooking.

Spices

• Spices should heighten the taste of the food, not overpower it. Use in moderation.


• Spices like paprika, chili, garlic and curry, lose their flavour/become bitter when you add them to bubbling hot oil or butter. They remain intact when lightly sautéed.


• Reserve special graters only for ginger or nutmeg, as their flavours can be easily transferred.
